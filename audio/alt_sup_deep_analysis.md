# Do ALT and SUP Make Sense? A Deep Corpus-Driven Analysis

*A critical examination of the Significance triad's two most contested operators*

---

## Executive Summary

ALT and SUP are the right *conceptual slots* in the ontology. The 3x3x3 architecture demands them: the Significance triad needs a ground (differentiating within meaning) and a figure (relating within meaning). The empirical question is whether the *names* "Alternation" and "Superposition" carve language at its actual joints, or whether better verbs exist.

**The verdict:** The operators are structurally necessary and empirically validated. But the names create a systematic parsing problem that the corpus data exposes precisely. "Alternation" undersells ALT's actual function; "Superposition" borrows physics jargon that conflates the operator with one of its application domains. Both names could be sharper.

---

## 1. What the Corpus Actually Shows

### 1.1 ALT and SUP Dominate Conceptually Dense Text

In Corpus B (philosophy, quantum physics, wisdom literature), ALT and SUP together account for **49.0%** of all consensus classifications:

| Operator | Consensus Count | % of All |
|----------|---------------:|--------:|
| SUP | 397 | 29.5% |
| ALT | 262 | 19.5% |
| CON | 261 | 19.4% |
| INS | 119 | 8.8% |
| SEG | 118 | 8.8% |

This isn't noise. Philosophy and physics don't primarily describe or connect things. They *distinguish between interpretations* (ALT) and *hold multiple valid frameworks simultaneously* (SUP). The operators capture something real about how meaning-making works in demanding registers.

### 1.2 The ALT-SUP Confusion Zone Is Precisely Localized

When Claude and GPT-4 disagree between ALT and SUP, the confusion has a remarkably clean signature:

- **210 contested clauses** (107 Claude=ALT/GPT-4=SUP, 103 the reverse)
- **100% agreement on Q2 (Domain = SIGNIFICANCE)** in every single contested case
- **100% disagreement on Q1 (Mode):** Claude says DIFFERENTIATING, GPT-4 says RELATING, or vice versa
- **Cross-confusion rate: 17.6%** of all clauses where either model picks ALT or SUP

The models never confuse ALT/SUP with operators outside the Significance triad on the Domain axis. The disagreement is entirely about whether a clause is *differentiating within meaning* or *relating within meaning*. This tells us something important: **the Domain axis (Significance) is secure. The Mode axis (Differentiating vs Relating) is the soft boundary.**

### 1.3 The Confusion Is Not Random

Looking at the contested examples reveals a clear pattern:

**Claude calls it ALT (differentiating) when GPT-4 calls it SUP (relating):**
> "The entangled Schrodinger cat state obtained immediately upon measurement of a superposed two-state quantum system is often considered paradoxical because it appears to predict two..."

> "In other words, entanglement is a non-commutative phenomenon."

> "The quantum measurement problem still finds no consensus."

**Claude calls it SUP (relating) when GPT-4 calls it ALT (differentiating):**
> "Superpositions must be considered when one cannot distinguish between possible paths, i.e., histories, leading to the current state of the system."

> "Since different classical states are orthogonal, the claim implies no non-trivial superpositions can be observed in classical systems."

> "We consider the characterization of entanglement from the perspective of a Heisenberg formalism."

The pattern: sentences *about* quantum superposition or entanglement are systematically ambiguous between ALT and SUP because the *subject matter* describes phenomena that involve both differentiating interpretations and holding multiple simultaneous states. The operator system is being asked to classify sentences whose semantic content *is itself the boundary between the two operators.*

This is actually strong evidence that ALT and SUP carve a real joint. If they didn't, the confusion would be scattered. Instead, it clusters exactly at the theoretical boundary.

## 2. Do ALT and SUP Carve at the Right Joints?

### 2.1 The Structural Argument (Strong)

The 3x3x3 architecture requires exactly three operators in the Significance triad, playing three roles:

| Role | What It Does | Current Name |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Ground** | The condition for meaning-change to happen | ALT |
| **Figure** | What emerges from that ground | SUP |
| **Pattern** | What makes it intelligible/persistent | REC |

The dependency ordering is: ALT -> SUP -> REC. You need values to change before you can have conflict. You need conflict before you can restructure the interpretive frame itself.

This is structurally isomorphic to:
- **Existence:** NUL (ground: void) -> SIG (figure: distinction) -> INS (pattern: instance)
- **Structure:** SEG (ground: boundary) -> CON (figure: connection) -> SYN (pattern: whole)
- **Significance:** ALT (ground: change) -> SUP (figure: multiplicity) -> REC (pattern: self-recognition)

The slots are mandatory. Something must serve as the ground of meaning-change, and something must serve as meaning-multiplicity. The question is whether ALT and SUP name those slots well.

### 2.2 The Presupposition Argument (Strong)

The technical manual's strongest claim: reversing ALT -> SUP produces biological impossibility. Trying to hold superposition before alternation is "a nervous system attempting to maintain multiple simultaneous states before it can switch between them -- the definition of seizure."

This checks out. In the corpus:
- ALT exemplars are about **distinguishing**, **negating**, **contrasting**: "Reality is often otherwise," "You people think it is race, but it is not," "This interpretation contradicts both the theory and observed outcomes."
- SUP exemplars are about **co-holding**, **defining multiply**, **inhabiting paradox**: "This is called piety and one who acts upon it is called pious," "All meanings are mutually related," "As we ask, so we shall receive."

You need to distinguish between states before you can hold them simultaneously. The presupposition ordering is real.

### 2.3 The Naming Problem (Real)

Here's where the trouble starts.

**"Alternation"** -- the name -- implies *switching between* states. Toggle. Flip. But the corpus exemplars show ALT doing something broader: *distinguishing within an interpretive frame*. The top ALT exemplar across 41 languages is a sentence denying a claim ("They said what Captain Younis Khan was upset about is not correct"). That's not switching or toggling. It's drawing a distinction within meaning-space.

The theoretical note acknowledges this: ALT is grounded in Husserl's time-consciousness, the "flowing succession of alternating nows." But in practice, the corpus shows ALT functioning as **interpretive differentiation** -- not frame-*switching* but frame-*distinguishing*.

**"Superposition"** -- the name -- borrows from quantum mechanics. This creates a double problem:
1. It makes the operator sound like it's only about physics
2. When classifying sentences *about* quantum superposition, models can't tell whether the clause is performing SUP (holding multiplicity) or merely describing it

The corpus shows SUP actually functioning as **interpretive co-validity** -- the capacity to hold multiple meanings as simultaneously valid. "Superposition" captures this for the physics case but undersells it everywhere else.

## 3. What Are the Better Verbs?

### 3.1 Criteria for Better Names

A better name for each operator should:
1. **Describe what the operator does, not one application domain**
2. **Distinguish clearly from adjacent operators** (minimize the 17.6% confusion)
3. **Preserve the presupposition ordering** (ground before figure)
4. **Work across registers** (everyday language, philosophy, physics, data systems)

### 3.2 Candidates for ALT

| Candidate | Pros | Cons |
|-----------|------|------|
| **Alternation** (current) | Established; captures temporal oscillation | Implies switching, not distinguishing; too narrow |
| **Distinction** | Matches corpus exemplars perfectly; Bateson's "difference that makes a difference" | Could be confused with SIG (which designates) or SEG (which partitions) |
| **Differentiation** | Exact technical term for Mode=DIFFERENTIATING x Domain=SIGNIFICANCE | Already used for the Mode axis itself; would be circular |
| **Revaluation** | Captures value-change within meaning | Sounds like financial operations |
| **Inflection** | Captures the turning/bending of meaning without breaking it | Grammatical associations may confuse |
| **Divergence** | Captures how interpretations split apart | Sounds destructive; doesn't preserve frame-stability |
| **Contrast** | Exactly what the corpus shows ALT doing; cross-linguistically stable | Passive; doesn't suggest an operation |
| **Variation** | Musical/biological overtones; preserves frame while changing content | Too generic; could mean anything |

**Recommendation: Keep ALT.** The abbreviation is fine. The long form "Alternation" is adequate. But the *gloss* should change from "frame-switching" to **"interpretive distinction"** or **"value-differentiation within meaning."** The theoretical note already says this -- the corpus confirms it. The exemplars are about *distinguishing*, *denying*, *contrasting*, *reframing* -- not about switching a toggle.

### 3.3 Candidates for SUP

| Candidate | Pros | Cons |
|-----------|------|------|
| **Superposition** (current) | Established; captures physics case well; radical departure from tradition | Domain-specific jargon; conflates operator with application |
| **Ambivalence** | Captures the holding-without-resolving; emotionally precise | Connotates indecision rather than co-validity |
| **Multiplicity** | Direct description of what the operator produces | Sounds like counting; doesn't capture the *tension* of co-validity |
| **Coexistence** | Neutral, clear, cross-domain | Bland; doesn't capture that the coexisting states are in *tension* |
| **Confluence** | Multiple streams meeting without merging | Could be confused with SYN (which does merge) |
| **Polyphony** | Multiple voices, all valid, none dominant; Bakhtin | Musical metaphor may not travel; could sound precious |
| **Indeterminacy** | Captures the unresolved nature; philosophically precise | Suggests the values are *unknown* rather than multiply-known |
| **Contention** | Captures the productive tension between co-valid states | Aggressive connotation; sounds like conflict, not co-validity |

**Recommendation: Keep SUP, but refine the gloss.** "Superposition" is doing real work. Its strangeness is a feature -- it signals that this operator is EO's most radical departure. But the gloss should be **"interpretive co-validity"** or **"simultaneous multiplicity,"** not "holding contradictions." The corpus shows SUP as often non-contradictory: systems of definition coexisting, multiple valid frameworks operating in parallel, perspectival multiplicity. "Contradiction" is just one species of SUP.

## 4. The Deeper Issue: The Q1 Boundary

The 17.6% cross-confusion between ALT and SUP is not a naming problem. It's a **Mode problem**. Both operators live in SIGNIFICANCE. The only thing separating them is whether the clause is DIFFERENTIATING (pulling apart) or RELATING (holding together).

But many sentences in the Significance triad do *both at once*:
- "The quantum measurement problem still finds no consensus" -- differentiates by stating failure, relates by asserting the coexistence of competing views
- "In other words, entanglement is a non-commutative phenomenon" -- differentiates by recharacterizing, relates by equating two descriptions

This isn't a flaw in ALT/SUP. It's a property of the Significance domain itself. In Existence and Structure, differentiating and relating are clearly distinct operations (you either split something or connect it). In Significance, the act of distinguishing interpretations and the act of holding them in tension are **co-constitutive** -- you can't distinguish meanings without simultaneously holding them for comparison, and you can't hold multiple meanings without having distinguished them.

The technical manual already says this: "Within any single turn, the three operators are co-constitutive -- they co-arise as aspects of one triadic event." The 17.6% confusion is the co-constitution showing up empirically.

## 5. What About the Verb "DEF" in the Wiki?

The wiki content uses "DEF" for what the technical manual calls "SUP." This is interesting -- "DEF" (from "Defiance"? "Deferral"?) would emphasize the operator's refusal to resolve, rather than its simultaneous-holding. But it doesn't appear in the type system (`EOOp`), the constants, or the corpus analysis. It may be a wiki-specific renaming experiment that hasn't propagated.

If "DEF" means **Deferral**, it captures something the corpus shows clearly: SUP is not just about holding multiplicity, it's about *refusing premature resolution*. The caseworker scenario makes this vivid -- the system should be able to say "both values are currently valid; we defer resolution." But "Deferral" loses the productive tension. Superposition is not just waiting to decide. It's holding incompatible states as simultaneously informative.

## 6. Concrete Recommendations

### 6.1 Keep the operator codes ALT and SUP

They're embedded in the type system, the constants, the event schema, the corpus data. Changing them would be a breaking change for no structural benefit.

### 6.2 Sharpen the glosses

| Current | Proposed |
|---------|----------|
| ALT = "Alternation (frame-switching)" | ALT = "Alternation (interpretive distinction)" |
| SUP = "Superposition (holding contradictions)" | SUP = "Superposition (simultaneous co-validity)" |

The proposed glosses match what the corpus *actually shows* these operators doing across 42 languages. "Frame-switching" is too narrow for ALT. "Holding contradictions" is too narrow for SUP.

### 6.3 Address the Q1 boundary in classification prompts

The 17.6% ALT-SUP confusion happens because classifiers can't reliably distinguish DIFFERENTIATING from RELATING within the Significance domain. Two interventions:

1. **Add a disambiguation heuristic:** "If the clause asserts that two interpretations *differ* or one is *wrong*, it's ALT. If the clause asserts that two interpretations *coexist* without resolution, it's SUP."

2. **Accept co-constitution in edge cases:** Some clauses genuinely perform both ALT and SUP simultaneously. Rather than forcing a choice, the classification system could flag these as "Significance-triad-unresolved" and let the Object axis (Q3) carry the discriminative weight. The Object axis already achieves z=+7.87 in specialist text -- it's the strongest signal available.

### 6.4 Do not replace with domain-specific terms

Neither "Reinterpretation" (too cognitive), "Toggle" (too mechanical), "Quantum superposition" (too domain-locked), nor "Ambiguity" (too vague) would improve on ALT/SUP. The current names have the advantage of being *slightly odd* -- they force users to learn what EO means by them, rather than projecting prior associations.
